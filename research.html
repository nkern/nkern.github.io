Research

My research interests lie at the interface of data analysis and theory of high redshift galaxy and structure formation. I am particularly interested in the development of analysis pipelines for intensity mapping experiments, and the connection of data to astrophysical and cosmological models. See below for some of my recent and ongoing projects.

First Limits on the 21cm Power Spectrum from HERA Phase I

HERA Collaboration in prep.
I am leading the analysis of HERA Phase I data en route to producing its first upper limit on the 21cm power spectrum at the EoR. More details on this to come shortly.

Absolute Calibration Strategies for the Hydrogen Epoch of Reionization Array

Kern et al. in prep.
Precision calibration is fundamental to 21cm science. In this paper, we investigate various approaches for calibration of the HERA instrument, including sky-based, redundant, and hybrid calibration schemes. For foreground avoidance power spectrum estimators, we show that HERA can be well-calibrated with a small number of degrees of freedom. We also show that, when not handled properly, calibration can actually excerbate inherent instrumental contaminants and prohibit a detection of the 21cm signal. This is particularly important for radio interferometric experiments with redundant and closely packed antennas.

Mitigation of Internal Instrumental Coupling for 21cm Cosmology

Kern et al. 2019a | Accepted to ApJ | arxiv:1909.11732
Instrumental systematics are a persistent problem for 21cm radio telescopes, and can fundamentally limit 21cm science. In this paper, we analytically describe the phenomenology of common radio instrumental systematics like cable reflections and crosstalk. We propose novel algorithms for modeling and removing these systematic in the data, and use numerical visibility simulations to validate their performance and demonstrate their resistance to signal loss. These techniques are general and applicable to most if not all intensity mapping experiments.






Kern et al. 2019b | In Review in ApJ | arxiv:1909.11733
In this work, we investigate the HERA Phase I system for instrumental systematics. We detail the properties of HERA's cable reflection and crosstalk systematics, which if not mitigated would severly hinder if not outright prohibit HERA's ability to detect the 21cm signal. We then apply the systematic modeling techniques described in Paper I to a sample of HERA data as a proof-of-concept, showing we can suppress systematics down to six orders of magnitude in dynamic range with respect to the bright foregrounds in the power spectrum. This allows us to at least partially recover spatial modes that were contaminated by instrumental systematics, and may be a crucial factor for early power spectrum detections with HERA.

Cosmic Dawn Parameter Estimation with Emulators

Kern et al. 2017 | ApJ, 848, 23 | arxiv:1705.04688
Connecting data to theoretical predictions from sophisticated and compute-intensive simulations is often prohibitively expensive when using MCMC sampling methods. A solution is to use machine learning methods to emulate the mapping of the simulation's input parameters to its output products with a less computationally expensive surrogate model. We explored this in the context of a cosmological 21cm simulation, and demonstrated a parameter constraint forecast jointly across cosmological LCDM, EoR star formation and Cosmic Dawn X-ray heating parametrs using a full MCMC sampler and a 1000-hour mock HERA observation. Machine learning techniques like emulation are crucial for enabling parameter inference with expensive, cosmological simulations of Cosmic Dawn.
