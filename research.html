Research

My research interests lie at the interface of data analysis and theory of high redshift galaxy and structure formation. Broadly, I am interested in cosmological data analysis, optimal signal extraction, radio interferometry, star and galaxy formation, and cosmological large scale structure. I am particularly interested in the development of analysis pipelines for intensity mapping experiments, and the robust connection of data to cosmological models. See below for some of my recent and ongoing projects and their selected publications. See my ADS library for a full list of publications.

Improved z=8 Limits on the 21 cm Power Spectrum from HERA Phase I

HERA 2022a (lead author: Kern) | ApJ, 925, 221 | arxiv:2108.02263

The Hydrogen Epoch of Reionization Array (HERA) is a new radio interferometer that aims to detect the 21 cm signal from cosmic hydrogen at the Epoch of Reionization and Cosmic Dawn. I led the first analysis of HERA Phase I observations, which set new upper limits on the power spectrum at z=8 and z=10.4 (pink triangles). Our z=8 limits improved upon existing limits by over an order of magnitude in sensitivity. These gains were due largely by our analysis pipeline's careful accounting of systematics, and the development of new tools for systematic mitigation that I led (see below). Read more about this result at MIT news, the SKA magazine and a press release.






HERA Collaboration 2022b | ApJ, 924, 51 | arxiv:2108.07282

This companion paper used a suite of theoretical methods to interpret HERA's first power spectrum upper limits and set constraints on the properties of the intergalactic medium (IGM) at redshifts 7.9 and 10.4. Principally, this analysis shows that the HERA limits require the IGM to be heated above its adiabatic cooling limit by z=7.9 (purple arrow). This implies that early X-ray sources from the first galaxies (e.g. from high-mass X-ray binaries) must have been more luminous than their local-universe analogues, and is consistent with a population of low-metallicity HMXB. This is the most stringent constraint on the X-ray properties of compact objects at Cosmic Dawn to-date.

Understanding the Limits of Gaussian Process Based Foreground Modeling

Kern et al. 2021 | MNRAS,501,1463K | arxiv:2010.15892

The scientific potential of 21 cm cosmology is currently hindered largely by bright galactic and extragalactic foreground emission. Many techniques have been proposed to model and subtract such foregrounds, although none of proven sufficient on real data to yield a detection of the 21 cm power spectrum. In this paper, we revisit a popular technique for foreground modeling using Gaussian processes (GP), and show that such a data model is in fact closely related to the widely studied inverse covariance model used in CMB and optical galaxy surveys. Furthermore, we show that improper normalization of a GP-based data model can lead to non-negligible bias in the recovered power spectrum. We show how one can mitigate this bias by compromising overall sensitivity.

Absolute Calibration Strategies for the Hydrogen Epoch of Reionization Array

Kern et al. 2020b | ApJ, 890, 122 | arxiv:1910.12943

Precision calibration is fundamental to 21 cm science. In this paper, we investigate various approaches for calibration of the HERA instrument, including sky-based and hybrid redundant & absolute calibration schemes. For foreground avoidance power spectrum estimators, we show that HERA can be well-calibrated with a small number of degrees of freedom across time and frequency. We also show that, when not handled properly, calibration can actually excerbate inherent instrumental contaminants in the data. We further describe calibration gain smoothing techniques for mitigating this effect. These considerations may be particularly important for radio interferometric experiments with redundant and closely packed antennas like HERA.

Mitigation of Internal Instrumental Coupling for 21cm Cosmology

Kern et al. 2019 | ApJ, 884, 105 | arxiv:1909.11732

Instrumental systematics are a persistent problem for 21cm radio telescopes, and can fundamentally limit 21cm science. In this paper, we analytically describe the phenomenology of common radio instrumental systematics like cable reflections and crosstalk. We propose novel algorithms for modeling and removing these systematic in the data, and use numerical visibility simulations to validate their performance and demonstrate their resistance to signal loss. These techniques are general and applicable to most if not all intensity mapping experiments.






Kern et al. 2020a | ApJ, 888, 70 | arxiv:1909.11733

In this work, we investigate the HERA Phase I system for instrumental systematics. We detail the properties of HERA's cable reflection and crosstalk systematics, which if not mitigated would severly hinder if not outright prohibit HERA's ability to detect the 21cm signal. We then apply the systematic modeling techniques described in Paper I to a sample of HERA data as a proof-of-concept, showing we can suppress systematics by two orders of magnitude down to the noise floor of nightly observation. This allows us to at least partially recover spatial modes that were contaminated by instrumental systematics, and may be a crucial factor for early power spectrum detections with HERA.

Cosmic Dawn Parameter Estimation with Emulators

Kern et al. 2017 | ApJ, 848, 23 | arxiv:1705.04688

Connecting data to theoretical predictions from sophisticated and compute-intensive simulations is often prohibitively expensive when using MCMC sampling methods. A solution is to use machine learning methods to emulate the mapping of the simulation's input parameters to its output products with a less computationally expensive surrogate model. We explored this in the context of a cosmological 21cm simulation, and demonstrated a parameter constraint forecast jointly across cosmological LCDM, EoR star formation and Cosmic Dawn X-ray heating parametrs using a full MCMC sampler and a 1000-hour mock HERA observation. Machine learning techniques like emulation are crucial for enabling parameter inference with expensive, cosmological simulations of Cosmic Dawn.
