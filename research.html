21cm cosmology at the epoch of reionization

My research explores the frontiers of the high redshift universe through radio observations of primordial hydrogen. I am interested in using these as a probe to study how the first generation of stars and galaxies formed and interacted with their environments, and the ways this process was tied to the large-scale structure of the universe.



galaxy clusters

As an undergrad student, I worked with professor Chris Miller researching the dynamics of galaxy clusters. There are a few ways to derive the masses of galaxy clusters, however, each method is subject to certain weaknesses and uncertainties. The development of multiple techniques that can each derive galaxy cluster masses and are consistent with each other is therefore crucial to understand the large scale clustering of dark matter in our universe. One particular class of these techniques is called dynamical methods, which use the kinematic velocities and positions of galaxies orbiting a galaxy cluster's center of mass to trace out its gravitational potential. Within the spectrum of dynamical methods, we have been testing the limits of the Caustic Technique against N-body simulations, like the Millennium Simulation, to try and improve its ability to derive galaxy cluster masses with high precision and accuracy. Simulations provide a unique opportunity for us to test our algorithms, in that, unlike the real universe, we know what the "real mass" of a simulated galaxy cluster, and can diagnose where our techinque fails and succeeds.

I worked on finding ways to increase the precision and accuracy of the Caustic Technique by implementing a stacking routine. Two of the major sources of uncertainty in the Caustic Technique are correlated to 1. low numbers of observed galaxies in a cluster, and 2. a cluster's deviation from spherical symmetry. I am working to see if we can combine multiple clusters' kinematic data together and then measure one averaged Caustic mass for the entire ensemble system. This routine is called a "stacking" technique, and it helps to alleviates these two major sources of uncertainty that plague the Caustic Technique. I found that with a stacking routine, we can measure an averaged Caustic mass to 30% better precision than an individual Caustic mass when galaxy sampling is sparse.
