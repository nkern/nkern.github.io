Nicholas Kern

MIT Pappalardo Fellow
Dept. of Physics and the MIT Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research



I am a Pappalardo Fellow at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in the Department of Physics and the Kavli Institute for Astrophysics and Space Research. I received my PhD in astrophysics at the University of California, Berkeley in 2020.

I am a data-focused cosmologist, working at the interface of radio observations, data analysis, and theoretical inference for 21 cm cosmology. I am an expert in developing novel tools for identifying weak signals in large, noisy, and contaminated datasets, and for making robust connections to astrophysical theory. Broadly, my research interests are in cosmological data analysis, radio interferometry, intensity mapping, star and galaxy formation, Bayesian parameter inference, and machine learning. See below for a summary of some of my recent work.

My current work focuses on developing a new and powerful approach for joint Bayesian inference of the 21 cm signal by leveraging advances in machine learning software and hardware to accelerate the otherwise computationally prohibitive posterior inference problem for 21 cm telescopes.

Research Highlights

HERA's Improved Limits on the EoR 21cm Power Spectrum

The Hydrogen Epoch of Reionization Array (HERA) is a new radio interferometer that aims to detect the 21 cm signal from cosmic hydrogen at the Epoch of Reionization and Cosmic Dawn. I led the first analysis of HERA Phase I observations, which set new upper limits on the power spectrum at z=7.9 and z=10.4 (HERA Collaboration 2022a). In particular, I led the development of HERA's precision systematic modeling algorithms (see below), which were critical in enablign HERA's improved measurements: our z=7.9 limits improved upon existing limits by over an order of magnitude. A companion study (HERA Collaboration 2022b) showed that these results require the intergalactic medium to be heated above its adiabatic cooling limit by redshift 8, and provide the first constraint on the X-ray efficiencies of high-mass X-ray binaries at this redshift. Read more about this result at MIT news, the SKA magazine and the press.











The analysis pipelines required for these limits, and 21 cm science in general, need to have precise control of systematics. To address these issues, I have worked on developing hybrid calibration schemes (Kern et al. 2020b), techniques for instrumental systematic identification, modeling and removal (Kern et al. 2019, Kern et al. 2020a), as well as methods for foreground modeling and subtraction (Kern et al. 2021).

Cosmic Dawn Parameter Estimation with Emulators

Connecting data to theoretical predictions from sophisticated and compute-intensive simulations is often prohibitively expensive using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) samplers. A solution is to use machine learning methods to emulate the mapping of the simulation's input parameters to its output products with a less computationally expensive surrogate model. We explored this in the context of a cosmological 21cm simulation, and demonstrated a parameter constraint forecast jointly across cosmological LCDM, EoR star formation and Cosmic Dawn X-ray heating parameters using a full MCMC sampler and a 1000-hour mock HERA observation (Kern et al. 2017).
